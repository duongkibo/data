## Emotional Direction per Beat

|---|---|---|

## Critical QC Lines

- **"Not his."** (Beat 1 Hook ending) — one breath, one low note, maximum stillness. The episode opens with these two words landing in complete silence.
- **"Three women. From his own company. On his company phone. On company time."** (Beat 4) — each sentence a separate cold fact. Not rushed, not performed. The attorney naming what she sees.
- **"She was an employment attorney. She knew exactly what that meant."** (Beat 4) — pause between the two sentences. The pause is the pivot point.
- **"I want the condo. And I want it done cleanly."** (Beat 6) — voice lift here. This is the turn.
- **"She went to the kitchen and made herself tea."** (Beat 6 ending) — maximum pause after. 3+ seconds. Let the audience receive it.
- **"How's the job search going?"** (Beat 7) — cool, controlled, the quietest form of power. Do not smile audibly into this line — it's flatter and more devastating that way.
- **"She didn't miss much at all."** (Epilogue) — pause 3+ seconds after this line. The breathing room at the end.
- **"The details always tell the truth. You just have to let yourself look."** (CTA) — deliberate, each word placed. Speaking to someone specific.

## Chunk 1 — Beats 1–4: Hook through Betrayal
**Save as:** `chunk1.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~4:15
**Approximate word count:** ~650 words

---

[knowing]

A notification. [pause 1s] One sentence. Twelve words. [pause 1s] "You have a new match!" [pause 2s] It came to her phone. [pause 1s] Not his.

[pause 2s]

[warm]

Nadia was thirty-six. An employment attorney at a boutique firm in a mid-sized city — the kind of lawyer who read the fine print not because she had to, but because she genuinely couldn't look away from the details everyone else missed. She had a reputation among colleagues: thorough, calm, the person you wanted in the room when everything was starting to fall apart.

She had been married to Marcus for five years. Together for seven. He was a director of business development at a tech company — charming in meetings, quick with a laugh, the kind of man who made you feel like the most interesting person in the room for exactly as long as he needed to.

They had a shared Apple ID from the early days of the relationship — back when everything was new enough that sharing felt easy. Marcus had mentioned, once or twice, that he should set up his own. He never did.

[pause 1s] That was the first mistake.

[foreboding]

It started on a Tuesday evening in March.

Nadia was in the kitchen making dinner when her phone buzzed on the counter. She glanced at the screen without picking it up. A notification bar. Orange-red icon she didn't recognize. The app name: Tinder. [pause 1s] The message: "You have a new match!" [pause 2s] And then — gone. The notification cleared itself before she could read more.

She stood at the stove for a moment. [pause 1s] Then she kept cooking.

But the thing about Nadia was that she didn't dismiss things. She filed them. Quietly. Accurately. And then she returned to them.

That night, while Marcus watched something in the living room, she searched the App Store. Tinder required its own device login — the notification had appeared through a background sync. Their shared Apple ID was pulling his account activity. He had logged in with his Apple credentials and never disabled notification mirroring on her device.

[pause 2s]

She opened his iCloud backup from her phone. [pause 1s] She had the Apple ID password. She had always had it. [pause 1s] He had simply forgotten that.

What she found was not one app. [pause 1s] It was three.

Tinder. Bumble. Hinge. All active. All showing recent messages. All timestamped across the last fourteen months. [pause 1s] Twenty-two unique matches. Conversations that went on for weeks. Two of them months.

She closed the screen. Put her phone face-down on the nightstand. [pause 1s] She didn't wake Marcus. She didn't say a word.

She lay in the dark and she thought. [pause 1s] The way she always thought before she acted on anything.

[whispers]

She gave herself three days before she went back in.

On Friday morning, while Marcus was at the gym, she sat at the kitchen table with her coffee and she went through everything. Systematically. The way you review evidence. Not searching for something to hurt herself with — documenting. Understanding the full shape of it before making a single move.

Tinder: fourteen months of activity. Forty-one conversations. Most brief. Several extended — including one that included a reservation at a hotel whose name she recognized from a work conference Marcus had attended. Except the conference had been two days. He'd stayed four.

Bumble: eleven months of activity. Nine conversations. Work-adjacent language in three of them — references to the same industry, the same company events.

Hinge: active for eleven months. Two conversations. One of them: a woman named Claire from his company's marketing team. Nadia knew the name. She had met Claire at Marcus's holiday party eleven months ago. Stood next to her at the drinks table for twenty minutes. Claire had been pleasant. [pause 2s] Talkative.

She did not cry.

She opened her laptop and pulled up his company's public LinkedIn org chart. Three names from his app conversations matched the org chart. Claire from marketing. A woman named Priya from partnerships. A third name she recognized from a team offsite he'd mentioned in passing.

[pause 2s]

Three women. [pause 1s] From his own company. [pause 1s] On his company phone. [pause 1s] On company time.

[pause 2s]

She was an employment attorney. [pause 1s] She knew exactly what that meant.

## Chunk 2 — Beats 5–7: Fall through Karma
**Save as:** `chunk2.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~5:10
**Approximate word count:** ~850 words

---

[heavy]

She kept the information to herself for three weeks.

This was not passivity. This was precision. She had seen, in her professional life, what happened when people acted before the evidence was fully assembled — cases that unraveled because someone couldn't wait, couldn't hold still, couldn't stop themselves from revealing what they knew before they were ready to use it. She was not going to make that mistake. Not in her own life.

She took screenshots. Detailed, timestamped, organized. She created a folder on a drive he couldn't access. She logged every match, every conversation thread she could access from the iCloud backup, every date, every duration.

She noted the hotel stay. She noted the conversations with his colleagues. She noted the timeline.

She called her sister once. Told her everything. Her sister said: *Nadia, you need to leave him. Today.* [pause 1s] Nadia said: *I'm going to. I'm just doing it correctly.*

She went to work. She handled her cases. She had lunch. She came home. She made dinner. She sat across the table from Marcus while he talked about his week. She listened. She answered when he asked her questions. She was present in the way that a person is present when they are also completely elsewhere.

[pause 1s] She was building something. And she was almost done.

[hopeful]

On week three, she made two moves.

The first was to her own attorney — a colleague she trusted, specializing in asset division and marital law. They met over lunch. She brought her folder. Her colleague looked at the documentation and said, with the particular quiet of someone who has seen this many times before: *This is very thorough. Where do you want to land?* [pause 1s]

Nadia said: *I want the condo. And I want it done cleanly.*

They had purchased the condo three years into the marriage. The down payment had come primarily from Nadia's savings — an inheritance from her grandmother, kept separate, documented. Her colleague confirmed: with proper documentation, the asset split would favor Nadia significantly. Marcus would leave with fair value but not the condo itself. [pause 1s] Nadia had already pulled the deed.

The second move was the one that would take longer to land. But it would land.

She anonymized the evidence. She removed names, stripped identifying details from every screenshot, reduced it to a pattern: company device usage, company time, company personnel. She drafted an email — precise, neutral, professional, the kind that employment departments open and do not close quickly. [pause 1s] She did not send it yet.

She waited until the divorce papers were ready. [pause 1s] Then she filed first.

Marcus came home to a served document and a wife who met him at the door and said, simply: [pause 1s] "I know about the apps. All three of them. I know about Claire. And Priya. I know about the Riverside Inn." [pause 2s] She stepped aside to let him in. "Your attorney will be in touch." [pause 1s] She went to the kitchen and made herself tea.

[satisfied]

He called her six times that night. She did not pick up.

He sent three texts. The first was shock. The second was an explanation she had no interest in. The third was — and she would describe it to her sister later as *the most revealing thing he'd ever said to her* — an accusation: that she had violated his privacy by accessing the backup. [pause 2s]

She forwarded his third text to her attorney. [pause 1s] Then she sent the anonymized HR email.

The subject line: *Potential policy concern — company device personal use.* [pause 1s] She attached the evidence. She signed it with a professional alias. She sent it to the HR director whose name she had found on the company's public contact page.

She did not name Marcus. She did not name Claire or Priya. She named no one. [pause 1s] She simply documented a pattern. And suggested the company may wish to audit device usage records for the relevant department.

HR conducted their own investigation. [pause 2s] This took four weeks.

Marcus was called into HR on a Wednesday morning. He was informed that a review of company device records had surfaced significant personal use — including activity on three dating applications — during work hours, over a thirteen-month period. That two employees from his direct professional network had been involved in conversations of a non-professional nature. That company policy on inter-office relationships had been violated on multiple documented occasions.

He was placed on administrative leave that afternoon. [pause 1s] He was terminated the following Monday.

Claire from marketing resigned before her own meeting. [pause 1s] Priya from partnerships resigned the same week — a letter citing personal reasons, submitted quietly, without drama.

[pause 2s]

The divorce finalized six weeks after the termination. Nadia kept the condo. Marcus received a settlement that was fair by the letter of the law and exactly as generous as he had been during five years of marriage. [pause 1s] Which was to say: not particularly.

He called one more time, three weeks after the divorce finalized. She answered. He asked if she had sent the HR email. [pause 2s]

She said: *I documented a pattern. I sent it to the appropriate party. Everything after that was the company's decision.* [pause 1s] She paused. *How's the job search going?*

She hung up. She did not call back.

## Chunk 3 — Beat 8 + Epilogue + CTA

**Estimated duration:** ~2:05
**Approximate word count:** ~370 words

[final]

Marcus moved into a one-bedroom apartment in a neighborhood he had previously described as not for him. He had no income and a divorce settlement that had depleted his savings to what was technically fair. He told mutual acquaintances that the company had gone through a restructuring. It was not entirely untrue. The restructuring had specifically included him.

He was last reported — through the mutual acquaintances Nadia no longer sought out — updating his LinkedIn with the word *consulting* and sending connection requests to people he had not spoken to in years. [pause 1s] The consulting appeared to consist, primarily, of waiting.

Claire and Priya had each landed elsewhere. No one asked where Nadia had landed. They already knew. [pause 1s] She was exactly where she'd been before, minus the noise.

[warm]

Nadia took a solo trip in September. A week on a coast she'd been meaning to visit for three years and never had — because there had always been something Marcus needed that week, some schedule that took priority over hers.

She turned off notifications for everything. Her phone sat in the hotel room most mornings while she walked. She ate at places she chose alone. She read a book she'd been carrying in her bag for two months. She finished it in four days.

On the last evening, she sat on the beach until the light was nearly gone. She watched the water. She didn't miss a single notification. [pause 1s] She didn't miss much at all.

[pause 3s]

She came back and she worked. She built. She slept well, which surprised her — she hadn't known, until it stopped, how much the weight of unanswered questions had cost her.

The condo felt like hers in a way it hadn't in years. She repainted the study. Sage green. Put her diplomas back on the wall.

[inviting]

If this story stayed with you — if you've ever felt something was wrong and told yourself you were probably overreacting — [pause 1s] you probably weren't.

The details always tell the truth. You just have to let yourself look. [pause 1s]

Subscribe to Karma Caught Up for a new story every week. [pause 1s] And if someone you know needed to hear this today — [pause 1s] send it to them.

[pause 2s] 🦋 Karma always finds its way back.
