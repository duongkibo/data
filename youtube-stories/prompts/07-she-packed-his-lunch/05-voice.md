## Emotional Direction per Beat

| Beat | Tag | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Beat 1 Hook | [knowing] | Slow, deliberate. The narrator knows something the audience doesn't yet. Let the pauses breathe. |
| Beat 2 Intro | [warm] | Warm, intimate. Like telling a story to a friend. Claire is good. She is real. |
| Beat 3 Crack | [foreboding] | Slight drop in warmth. Slower pacing. Something is off and the voice mirrors it. |
| Beat 4 Betrayal | [whispers] | Drop register significantly at "I usually toss it in the break room." Quiet devastation — not theatrical. |
| Beat 5 Fall | [heavy] | Measured, deliberate. She is thinking through every word. Not emotional — precise. |
| Beat 6 Pivot | [hopeful] | Voice lifts on "Do something that makes you impossible to ignore." Slight warmth returning. |
| Beat 7 Karma | [satisfied] | Calm satisfaction. Not triumphant — earned. Let the irony speak. |
| Beat 8 Downfall | [final] | Even, quiet, complete. No embellishment. "She walked away. She did not look back." |
| Epilogue | [warm] | Full warmth returned. Reflective. The way you end a story you loved telling. |
| CTA | [inviting] | Genuine, not salesy. Speaking to someone who needed this story. |

---

## Critical QC Lines

- **"I usually toss it in the break room for whoever wants it."** — Voice must DROP here. Quiet. Almost disbelieving. This is the peak betrayal line. Do not let it be read as normal narration.
- **"Do something that makes you impossible to ignore."** — Voice lifts. This is the turn. Let it land.
- **"Alone. Every day."** — Maximum pause after this. Breathing room. The ending note.
- **"Two thousand, five hundred and fifty-five lunches."** — Deliberate. Each comma is a breath.
- **"She walked away. She did not look back."** — Two separate sentences with full pause. Finality.

## Chunk 1 — Beats 1–4: Hook through Betrayal
**Save as:** `chunk1.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~4:20
**Approximate word count:** ~640 words

---

[knowing]

Seven years. Two thousand, five hundred and fifty-five lunches. She made every single one. [pause 1s] And she had no idea where they were really going. [pause 2s] Until one Tuesday.

[pause 2s]

[warm]

Claire was thirty-four. A school librarian in a mid-sized city where everyone knew everyone, but not everything. She was the kind of woman who remembered how you took your coffee, who sent cards on actual birthdays, who had a standing order at the farmers market for the exact bread her husband liked — sourdough, thick-cut, from a woman named Rosie who sold out by nine a.m.

Greg was thirty-seven. An attorney at a downtown firm. He left the house at seven-forty-five every morning with a brown paper bag that Claire had packed at six-thirty: turkey on sourdough, no mustard, green apple, and a handwritten note she stopped signing after year three because she figured he knew who it was from by now.

They'd been married six years. Together for seven. And Claire thought — truly believed — they were fine.

[pause 1s] They were not fine.

[foreboding]

It started the way these things always start. Slowly. Then all at once.

February. Greg started staying late at the office on Tuesdays and Thursdays. Then it became most weekdays. Claire made dinner. Greg ate, but distracted — phone face-down on the table, one eye always on the notification screen.

She noticed. Of course she noticed. But she talked herself out of it. [pause 1s] He was under pressure at work. A big case. A difficult client. She knew better than to be that wife — the suspicious one, the needy one, the one who checked his phone.

Then in March, she found a restaurant receipt in the pocket of his work pants. She always did his laundry. She almost always checked the pockets.

Chez Lune. A French place downtown. Forty-three dollars for lunch. A Tuesday.

For two.

[pause 2s]

She stared at it for a long time. Forty-three dollars for two people. A weekday. Chez Lune had a prix fixe. Three courses. You didn't go there to grab a quick bite. You went there to impress someone.

She put it back in his pocket. Washed his pants. Folded them. Put them on his side of the closet.

And the next morning, at six-thirty, she made his lunch. [pause 1s] Turkey on sourdough. No mustard. Green apple. A brown paper bag she folded down twice at the top, the way he'd once told her he liked it.

She put it in his hand at seven-forty-five. He kissed her cheek. He walked out the door.

She stood in the kitchen for a moment. [pause 1s] Something had shifted. She just didn't know what yet.

[whispers]

She did what she told herself she'd never do. On a Tuesday night in April, while Greg was in the shower, she picked up his phone.

She wasn't even sure what she was looking for. [pause 1s]

She found it in the first thirty seconds.

Natalie. A paralegal at his firm. The thread went back fourteen months. The messages were not ambiguous. They were not work messages. They were — [pause 1s] they were the kind of messages you write to someone when you think you're in love with them. When you've decided that what you have at home is not enough.

Claire scrolled back. Slowly. [pause 1s]

And then she found the part that broke something she hadn't known was still whole.

Natalie had asked, last September, what Greg was doing for lunch. And Greg had written: *Just the usual. Claire packs me a bag. I usually toss it in the break room for whoever wants it.*

[pause 2s]

She put the phone down. Exactly where she found it. Screen down.

She went to the kitchen. Sat at the table. [pause 1s] In the dark.

Two thousand, one hundred and ninety-one lunches by her rough count — she'd done the math before, as a sort of funny milestone. And at some point — she didn't know when — they had been going directly into a trash can in an office break room thirty floors above this city. [pause 1s]

Greg came out of the shower. Said something. She answered. She didn't remember what. She was somewhere else entirely.

## Chunk 2 — Beats 5–7: Fall through Karma Strikes
**Save as:** `chunk2.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~5:00
**Approximate word count:** ~820 words

---

[heavy]

April became May. Claire kept making the lunches.

She understood, later, why she did it. It was the only thing that felt real. The ritual of it. Six-thirty. Turkey. Sourdough. Green apple. Fold the bag twice. [pause 1s] It was the last thing she could control in a life that had gone quietly underwater.

She went to work. She checked out books. She helped second-graders find stories. She smiled. She answered emails. She made dinner. She slept on her side of the bed.

She did not confront Greg.

Some people do, immediately. Some people scream, throw things, call the other woman, send screenshots to his mother. [pause 1s] Claire was not some people. Claire was thirty-four years old, standing in her kitchen at six-thirty every morning, making a sandwich for a man who threw it in the garbage — and she was thinking.

She was thinking very carefully.

She called her sister. Once. For two hours. Her sister said: *You need to leave him.* [pause 1s] Claire said: *I know. But I need to do this right.*

That was May seventh.

She spent May planning. [pause 1s] Quietly. Calmly. The way you plan something that matters.

[hopeful]

Her Aunt Dorothy died that April.

They had not been close. Dorothy was her father's older sister — eccentric, twice-divorced, ran a catering business out of a farmhouse in Vermont for thirty years. Claire had visited twice as a child. She remembered the smell of Dorothy's kitchen. Bread, always bread. Something with thyme.

Dorothy left Claire forty-two thousand dollars and a handwritten note that said: *Do something that makes you impossible to ignore.*

[pause 2s]

Claire read that note in May. While she was planning.

She had spent the previous six months, while packing Greg's lunches, listening to a culinary podcast called The Line. She didn't know when it started. It just — it made her feel something when nothing else did.

She looked into culinary programs. Found a twelve-week professional course at a community college twenty minutes from the house. She enrolled in June. She did not tell Greg. She paid with Aunt Dorothy's money.

She also found a food truck for sale. [pause 1s] Forty thousand dollars. Used. White. Needed a paint job.

She filed for divorce in July. Greg didn't see it coming. [pause 1s] He genuinely didn't. He sat across from her at the kitchen table and actually said: *But things have been so good lately.*

[pause 2s]

Claire did not say anything about Natalie. She did not say anything about the receipt. Or the phone. Or the break room trash can. [pause 1s] She said: *I just need more.*

She moved out that weekend. Painted the food truck herself. Pale yellow. And she named it. [pause 1s]

Two Thousand Five Hundred and Fifty-Five.

[satisfied]

The food truck launched in September. A Tuesday.

Claire's menu was simple. Sandwiches. Six varieties. One was called The Usual: turkey on sourdough, no mustard, green apple sliced thin. She put it at the top. Nine dollars.

She set up outside the farmers market on Saturday mornings, near Rosie's bread stand. Rosie gave her a discount on sourdough. Claire gave Rosie free lunch on Saturdays.

The first week she sold out by noon. [pause 1s] The second week she had a line.

A food blogger found her in October. Took a photo of the truck name and asked what it meant. Claire told her. Not all of it. Just: *That's how many lunches I made for someone who didn't appreciate them. So I decided to make them for people who do.*

The post got twelve thousand likes in forty-eight hours. [pause 1s]

Local news came the week after. Then a regional magazine. Then a piece in a food newsletter with four hundred thousand subscribers. The headline: *She Packed Lunches for Years. Now She's Packing Houses.*

[pause 1s]

Meanwhile.

Greg did not immediately understand what had happened to his life. But it started becoming clear that fall.

Natalie ended things in August. Not because she found out about the divorce — she already knew. She ended it because Greg had become, as she told a mutual colleague, *exhausting.* She had thought he was sophisticated. She had not understood that what she was attracted to was a performance. And the performance required an audience. [pause 1s] She had no interest in the man underneath it.

By November, Greg's firm went through a restructuring. Eleven attorneys were let go. [pause 1s] Greg was one of them.

He found a smaller position at a different firm. Less prestigious. He had to explain, in interviews, why he'd left. He used the word *downsizing.* It was not entirely untrue.

He called Claire in October. She did not pick up. He left a voicemail that she described to her sister as *a lot of words that added up to nothing.* [pause 1s] She did not call him back.

He called again in December. She did not pick up.

She was, by December, in conversation with a small investor group who had heard about the food truck. [pause 1s] They wanted to talk about a brick-and-mortar location.

## Chunk 3 — Beat 8 + Epilogue + CTA: Downfall through Subscribe
**Save as:** `chunk3.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~2:10
**Approximate word count:** ~380 words

---

[final]

The restaurant opened the following March. Small. Twelve tables. Corner location two blocks from the farmers market. She called it Dorothy's.

The menu was the same as the truck. Simple. Good. The Usual: still nine dollars, still at the top.

[pause 1s]

Greg found out through a mutual friend who sent him the Instagram link. He showed up on a Wednesday afternoon in April — one year, almost exactly, from the Tuesday she'd found the receipt.

He sat down. Claire was working the floor. She walked over.

She said: *Hi, Greg. What can I get you?* [pause 1s]

He ordered The Usual.

She brought it herself. Turkey on sourdough. No mustard. Green apple sliced thin. She set it in front of him. Then she placed the check beside it. Twelve dollars. [pause 1s] Prices had gone up.

She walked away. She did not look back.

[warm]

Claire still makes lunches.

Every morning before the restaurant opens, she preps. She's told interviewers that the ritual is what keeps her grounded. The repetition. The care. The attention to small things. [pause 1s] She says: *A good sandwich is just love with a crust.*

She has seven full-time staff now. Three of them are single mothers. She built the schedule around school pickup.

Her sister comes in on Sunday mornings. They eat at the corner table by the window and they don't talk about Greg. There's not much to say. [pause 1s] Some stories end not with a bang but with a check for twelve dollars and a walk back to the kitchen.

Aunt Dorothy's note is framed above the register. *Do something that makes you impossible to ignore.*

[pause 2s]

Claire found out, through a friend of a friend, that Greg eats lunch at his desk now. [pause 1s] Alone. Every day.

[inviting]

If this story stayed with you — if you've ever been the one doing all the small, invisible things and wondering if any of it mattered — [pause 1s] it mattered. It always mattered.

You just deserved someone who knew that. [pause 1s]

Subscribe to Karma Caught Up for a new story every week. [pause 1s] And if you know someone who needs to hear this today — [pause 1s] send it to them.

[pause 2s] 🦋 Karma always finds its way back.

## Voice QC Checklist

- [ ] "I usually toss it in the break room for whoever wants it." — register drops, near-whisper
- [ ] "Do something that makes you impossible to ignore." — voice lifts noticeably
- [ ] "Two Thousand Five Hundred and Fifty-Five." — every word deliberate, no rushing
- [ ] "Alone. Every day." — maximum pause after. Let it sit 3+ seconds before next line.
- [ ] "She walked away. She did not look back." — two full stops, no blending
- [ ] Beat 4 [whispers] section — consistently lower, quieter throughout
- [ ] Epilogue [warm] — full warmth restored, reflective pace
- [ ] All [pause Xs] honored — do not cut the silences
