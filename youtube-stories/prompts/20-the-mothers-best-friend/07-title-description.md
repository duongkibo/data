## Description (copy-paste ready)

```
She found a thank-you card in his mail.

It was addressed to Marcus. No return address. But the handwriting — elegant, looping, the specific way the capital M curved just slightly wider than it should — she had been reading it since she was seven years old.

It was Patricia's handwriting.

Patricia had been her mother Diane's best friend for twenty-two years. She had been in the front pew at Nina and Marcus's wedding. She had sat in the hospital waiting room the night their daughter Lily was born. She had brought a casserole the following Tuesday. And the Tuesday after that.

Nina called her mother that evening. Kept her voice light. Asked, casually, whether Patricia had been in touch with Marcus lately.

Her mother went quiet.

Two seconds.

That silence told her everything.

She drove to her mother's house that night. They sat at the kitchen table — the same table where Patricia had sat a hundred times — for four hours. Diane had noticed pieces. She hadn't assembled them. That night, they assembled them together. The betrayal was not just Nina's. It was Diane's too.

Nina called the family attorney the next morning. Her grandmother's house — pre-marital property, transferred to Nina's name alone two years before the marriage. Recorded. Claire confirmed it on a Thursday.

The divorce was filed on a Monday. Marcus was served at his office. She answered his call once: "Please use the attorneys." She hung up.

During discovery: a secondary credit card — opened quietly, used at restaurants, hotels, a jewelry store. Fourteen months.

Then came Patricia.

Diane made one phone call. She said four words: "I know. Don't call." She hung up. Patricia called back six times. Sent two texts. Diane did not respond to any of them.

The social network Patricia had relied on since her husband died — gone in one call.

Patricia and Marcus lasted five months. The weight of what it cost doesn't disappear. It accumulates.

Christmas that year: Nina, Lily, Diane, and Rachel — four women at the table. The seat that was his had been replaced by a potted olive tree in a terracotta pot. Diane said it needed more light.

Lily said it was pretty. Diane agreed.

After dinner, Nina helped her mother with the dishes. Her mother passed her a dish. She dried it. They didn't say much.

They didn't need to.

Karma always finds its way back. 🦋
```
