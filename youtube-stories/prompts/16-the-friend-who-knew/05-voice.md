## Emotional Direction per Beat

| Beat | Tag | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Beat 1 Hook | [knowing] | Slow, deliberate. The narrator knows the punchline before the audience does. Each sentence lands like a stone in still water. |
| Beat 2 Intro | [warm] | Warm and intimate — Iris is worth knowing. Make the audience love her before anything else happens. |
| Beat 3 Crack | [foreboding] | Temperature drops slightly. Slower pacing. The pattern of Thursdays narrated with a growing weight that the character doesn't yet fully name. |
| Beat 4 Betrayal | [whispers] | Drop register significantly at "She photographed them walking in." Quiet, controlled devastation — not theatrical. The calm of someone who has decided not to break. |
| Beat 5 Fall | [heavy] | Measured, deliberate. Not grief-stricken — planning. The sentences are precise. She is assembling something. |
| Beat 6 Pivot | [hopeful] | Voice lifts at "Let's begin." — this is the turn. Lift again at "Thursdays." — her one-word answer to Daniel. Let it land. |
| Beat 7 Karma | [satisfied] | Calm satisfaction. Not triumphant — earned. The irony of the karma speaks; don't over-read it. |
| Beat 8 Downfall | [final] | Even, quiet, complete. No embellishment. Events stated simply, as fact. |
| Epilogue | [warm] | Full warmth returned. The way you finish a story you loved telling. Reflective. |
| CTA | [inviting] | Genuine — not salesy. Speaking directly to someone who needed this. |

## Critical QC Lines

- **"Thursdays."** (Iris's one-word answer to Daniel, Beat 6) — maximum weight in minimum words. Low, even, final. Do not rush past this.
- **"She photographed them walking in. Timestamp visible."** (Beat 4) — voice must be near-flat here. Unnervingly calm. This is documentation not emotion.
- **"She drove to her sister's house. Had a glass of wine. Didn't say a single word about it."** (Beat 4) — each sentence its own full stop. The control in her.
- **"Thursdays are fine now."** (Epilogue) — maximum pause after. 3+ seconds. Let it breathe.
- **"That was the beginning of something."** (Epilogue) — warm, unhurried. Don't rush past the hopefulness.
- **"You were right. You always knew."** (CTA) — direct address. Meant.

## Chunk 1 — Beats 1–4: Hook through Betrayal
**Save as:** `chunk1.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~4:15
**Approximate word count:** ~650 words

---

[knowing]

She stood at the altar crying. She gave the speech. She held the bouquet. [pause 1s] She was also sleeping with the groom. [pause 2s] And she had been for almost two years.

[pause 2s]

[warm]

Iris was thirty-one. A graphic designer who worked from home in a third-floor apartment she'd decorated herself — plants everywhere, one long wall of bookshelves, a desk by the window that got the best morning light. She was methodical. She was loyal. She was the kind of person who remembered things about people they'd forgotten about themselves.

Maya had been her best friend since university. Fourteen years. The kind of friendship that had its own shorthand — a look across a room that meant a dozen things at once. Maya was the first call when anything happened. Good or bad.

Three years ago, Iris married Daniel. Maya was the maid of honor. She wrote the speech herself. She cried at the altar. She danced until one in the morning at the reception.

[pause 1s] Eight months after that wedding, she started sleeping with the groom.

[foreboding]

It started the way these things always do. Slowly. Then, once you saw it, all at once.

Iris noticed Thursdays first.

Maya had always been spontaneous — the friend who'd text at noon and expect you free by two. But around month eighteen of the marriage, Maya started canceling plans. Thursday dinners, specifically. Something came up. She was tired. She had a deadline. There was always a reason. Always plausible. Always on a Thursday.

Daniel worked late on Thursdays. He had for almost a year. Clients in a different time zone, he said. A project with a tight turnaround. She'd heard the explanation enough times that she'd stopped hearing it.

[pause 1s]

Then one Thursday in October, Iris drove past a restaurant near Daniel's office — the kind of place you go to when you want to be invisible in plain sight. She wasn't looking for anything. She just saw them. Through the window. Booth in the back. Close enough together that the body language required no interpretation.

She kept driving.

[pause 2s]

She drove to the end of the block. Parked. Sat in the car for eleven minutes. She knew this because she watched the clock the entire time.

Then she drove home. Made dinner. Waited.

Daniel came home at eight forty-five. He said the project ran late. She said she understood. [pause 1s] She was already thinking.

[whispers]

Iris didn't confront him that night. Or the next. She needed to be certain — not just of what she'd seen, but of what she was going to do with it.

She had a shared family tablet. Both their accounts were connected. It was used for travel bookings and calendar syncing — nothing secretive about it. She opened the location history. [pause 1s]

Six Thursdays. Same location. The Meridian Hotel on Calloway Street. Check-in between six and six-thirty. Departure around ten.

She pulled up Maya's Instagram. Checked her Thursday story archives — the ones Maya had posted so casually. Coffee. A candle. "Low key evening in." [pause 1s] Posted at six forty-five. From the Meridian Hotel on Calloway Street.

[pause 2s]

She did not cry.

She took screenshots. She checked the next Thursday. Daniel said he had a client dinner. She said she was going to her sister's for the evening. She drove to the Meridian parking lot at six-fifteen. Brought her camera. [pause 1s]

They arrived together at six twenty-two. Daniel held the door open for Maya the way he used to hold it for Iris. She photographed them walking in. Timestamp visible. She sat in her car and photographed the hotel entrance for fifty minutes.

She had everything she needed.

She drove to her sister's house. Had a glass of wine. Didn't say a single word about it. [pause 1s] She was not falling apart. She was assembling.

## Chunk 2 — Beats 5–7: Fall through Karma
**Save as:** `chunk2.mp3`
**Estimated duration:** ~5:10
**Approximate word count:** ~840 words

---

[heavy]

That weekend, she called her sister. Told her everything. All of it — the Thursdays, the restaurant window, the hotel, the timestamps, the photographs.

Her sister said: *Burn it all down right now.* [pause 1s] Iris said: *No. I'm going to do this correctly.*

She felt, in a way she couldn't quite describe, strangely clear. Not numb — clear. The fog that had been sitting at the edges of her marriage for eighteen months had simply lifted. There was no more wondering. No more explaining things away. [pause 1s] She knew. And knowing, it turned out, was easier than not knowing.

She had been carrying the weight of something she couldn't name for a year and a half. Now she could name it. [pause 1s] And she could set it down.

She went to work the next Monday. Designed a branding project for a client. Had lunch. Walked around the block at two. Came back. Finished the project. [pause 1s] Then she opened her browser and searched for divorce attorneys who specialized in marital asset protection.

She had owned the apartment before the marriage. [pause 1s] She scheduled a consultation for the following Wednesday.

[hopeful]

She chose an attorney named Helen — fifty-three, iron-grey hair, a manner like a surgeon: precise, unhurried, certain. Helen reviewed Iris's documentation in the first meeting and said very little. [pause 1s] Then she said: *You've done excellent work. Let's begin.*

Iris had owned the apartment outright before the marriage. The deed was in her name alone. She had paid the mortgage solo for two years before the wedding. Helen confirmed: under the terms of the state, the apartment was hers. Daniel would be leaving with his personal belongings and a settlement negotiated down to what was fair — not what was generous.

Iris filed quietly on a Tuesday morning. She did not tell Daniel. She did not tell Maya. She did not tell anyone except her sister and Helen. [pause 1s] She prepared for three weeks before the papers were served.

The day before they were served, she texted Maya. Just: *Hey, I've been thinking about you. Can we catch up Thursday?* [pause 1s]

Maya responded within four minutes. *Thursday's actually tough right now — can we do Friday?*

[pause 2s]

Iris replied: *Of course.* [pause 1s] She screenshot that too.

When Daniel was served papers the next morning, he called her three times in two hours. She answered the third call. He said — and these were his actual words — *Where did this come from?* [pause 1s]

Iris said: *Thursdays.*

She hung up. She did not call back.

[satisfied]

The divorce was finalized four months later.

The apartment stayed with Iris. Daniel moved to a short-term rental across town. He had the furniture from the second bedroom and a mediocre settlement that reflected exactly how much of the marital wealth he'd contributed — which, as Helen had quietly documented, was less than he'd believed. He had been counting on assets that weren't his to count on.

He did not contest. [pause 1s] Helen had been thorough.

Maya's fiancé was named Rowan. They had been engaged for eleven months. He was not part of Iris's plan — she had not gone to him, had not called him, had not sent him anything. [pause 1s] But small cities have small circles.

Rowan found out the way people find out these days: a mutual friend who knew someone who had seen something and, eventually, mentioned it to someone who knew him. The photographs existed. Screenshots existed. Once these things are real, they move.

Rowan ended the engagement publicly — not in the dramatic sense, but in the practical one. He called her family first. Then hers. He returned the ring through a mutual friend with a handwritten note that Iris was told about but never read. [pause 1s] She didn't need to.

Maya called Iris four days after the engagement ended.

Iris did not pick up.

Maya texted: *I need to explain. Please. I'm so sorry. I know I—*

[pause 2s]

Iris read it. Then she blocked the number. [pause 1s] There was nothing Maya could explain that Iris didn't already know in full.

Daniel, by then, had tried to reach Iris three more times. Two texts, one voicemail. The voicemail was long. [pause 1s] She listened to the first eighteen seconds and deleted it.

In the months after the divorce was finalized, something unexpected happened. The freelance clients Iris had been quietly building relationships with for three years — steadily, without urgency — began to come through all at once. A branding contract with a small hospitality group. A referral that turned into a six-month retainer. A design director from a firm she'd admired since graduate school reached out on a Thursday morning — the irony not lost on her — and offered her a part-time contract.

[pause 1s] She said yes.

By spring, she was working with five clients simultaneously. More than she'd ever managed alone. She'd converted the second bedroom — Daniel's former home office — into a proper studio. New desk. Better light. Her name, in her own handwriting, framed above the door.

## Chunk 3 — Beat 8 + Epilogue + CTA

**Estimated duration:** ~2:05
**Approximate word count:** ~370 words

[final]

Daniel and Maya lasted four months after the divorce.

It wasn't a clean ending. It was, by all accounts, a slow implosion — the kind that happens when two people who were each other's secret realize they don't actually work as each other's main event. The excitement, apparently, had required an audience. Without one, there wasn't much left.

[pause 1s]

They were last seen together at a mutual acquaintance's gathering — awkward, quieter than anyone remembered either of them being. Maya had cut her hair. Daniel had aged in the way men age when they've lost something they thought they owned. They left early, separately.

No one at that gathering had the heart to mention that Iris had just signed a lease on a studio apartment in a city two hours away. [pause 1s] A city with better light and no Thursdays that meant anything anymore.

[warm]

Iris moved in October.

The new apartment had three windows facing east and a building full of people she didn't know yet. She liked that. She set up her desk on the first day. Bought a plant from a market two blocks away. Put her name on the buzzer.

She joined a book group her new colleague mentioned — small, informal, met every other Tuesday at a wine bar nearby. She showed up. She listened. She remembered how people took their coffee.

Eighteen months after the divorce, at a mutual friend's dinner party, she was seated next to a man named Callum. He was a landscape architect. He asked questions and meant them. He remembered, the following week, something she'd mentioned once about a particular book.

[pause 1s] That was the beginning of something.

She told her sister, later: *The strange thing is, I was so afraid of losing fourteen years. And then I realized — I'd already been losing them for two of those years. I just hadn't been allowed to grieve it yet.*

[pause 2s] Thursdays are fine now.

[inviting]

If this story stayed with you — if you've ever been the one who noticed the small things and talked yourself out of trusting what you saw — [pause 1s] you were right. You always knew.

You just needed the clarity that was already coming. [pause 1s]

Subscribe to Karma Caught Up for a new story every week. [pause 1s] And if someone you know needed to hear this today — [pause 1s] send it to them.

[pause 2s] 🦋 Karma always finds its way back.
