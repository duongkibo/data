## CRITICAL PERFORMANCE NOTES

**The contributors slide (Beat 4 center):**
- "Project Contributors. [pause 1s] Eleven names. [pause 0.5s] Hers was fourth." — each clause lands separately. Do NOT smooth these into one breath. Each pause is a door closing.

**The elimination line (Beat 4 end):**
- "Her position eliminated. [pause 0.5s] Effective in thirty days." — deliver **slow**, like a sentence dropping into still water. Lower register. Let it sink before Charlotte moves on.

**The parking garage sequence (Beat 5):**
- The export sequence — Figma version history, Slack, Notion — should be delivered with **cold precision**, almost clinical. No anger. Just method. This is the most important tonal moment in the episode: Nadia is not falling apart. She is archiving.

**The UXPA line:**
- "U-X-P-A" — Charlotte must spell out each letter. If she runs them together as a word, re-gen that line separately and splice in CapCut.

## CHUNK 1 — Beats 1–3 + Beat 4 open (~640 words, ~4:20)

Paste NGUYÊN vào ElevenLabs:

```
[heavy] The platform launched on time. Under budget. The client was pleased. [pause 2s] And then the presentation began.

[pause 2s]

[warm] Nadia was thirty-two. A UX designer at Veltara, a mid-size software firm — the kind of place that prided itself on words like collaborative and meritocratic and people-first. She had been there six years. Promoted twice. Her last project before this one had reduced user drop-off by thirty-four percent. [pause 0.5s] The CEO had used that slide at three consecutive board meetings. Nadia's name was not on it. [pause 1s] She noticed. She filed it. She kept going.

[pause 2s]

[warm] The Orrin Group project was different in scale. A custom analytics platform — nine months of work, a client with real expectations, a scope that touched every department. [pause 0.5s] Nadia ran fourteen user interviews. She built three hundred Figma artboards. She attended eleven PM Thursdays in a row. [pause 1s] Her project manager was Marcus. He ran the meetings. He cc'd the stakeholders. He sent the recap emails signed with his name at the bottom.

[pause 2s]

[foreboding] She knew what that meant, the way you know what a low-pressure system means before it names itself. [pause 1s] She kept documenting. She kept designing. She kept her version history on.

[pause 2s]

[warm] The platform launched in Q4. On time. Under budget. [pause 0.5s] The Orrin Group sent a formal thank-you to Veltara leadership. The project came in clean, no revision cycles required, client satisfaction measured at ninety-one percent. [pause 1s] Nadia heard about the score in the same Slack channel as everyone else.

[pause 2s]

[foreboding] Three weeks after launch, Marcus presented to the executive team. Forty slides. [pause 0.5s] Nadia was in the room. She was not presenting. [pause 1s] She watched the slides go by — her interview framework, her information architecture, her design system, her user flow logic — attributed to "the team," to "our process," to "we identified." [pause 1s] Forty slides. [pause 0.5s] Her name appeared once. [pause 1s] In the footnote. Slide forty. Project Contributors. [pause 1s] Eleven names. [pause 0.5s] Hers was fourth.

[pause 2s]

[heavy] She did not speak during the Q-and-A. She had her notebook open. She took three lines of notes in her usual handwriting. [pause 0.5s] After the room cleared, she went back to her desk. She opened Figma. She looked at the version history panel. [pause 1s] Three hundred artboards. Her name on two hundred and eighty-three of them. Timestamped. Sequenced. The record of nine months of thinking, labeled with the date and the hand that made each decision. [pause 0.5s] She closed the panel. She went to lunch.

[pause 2s]

[foreboding] Two days later, Marcus filed a report with HR. Communication concerns. Team alignment issues. Unspecified friction during the project lifecycle. [pause 1s] HR called Nadia in on a Tuesday. [pause 0.5s] The conversation was eleven minutes long. The outcome was one sentence. [pause 2s]
```

## CHUNK 2 — Beat 4 cont. + Beats 5–7 (~840 words, ~5:45)

Paste NGUYÊN vào ElevenLabs:

```
[heavy] Her position eliminated. [pause 0.5s] Effective in thirty days.

[pause 2s]

[cold precision] She thanked them. She walked to the elevator. She rode to the parking garage on level two. [pause 1s] She sat in her car for twenty minutes. Not crying. Not calling anyone. [pause 0.5s] Thinking about what she had and what could be verified and what the version history already knew.

[pause 2s]

[cold precision] On day one she began exporting. [pause 0.5s] Figma version history — every artboard, every edit, every timestamp showing her name beside the change. [pause 0.5s] Slack — the threads where she'd raised the design decisions, the messages where Marcus had forwarded her work under his own signature. [pause 0.5s] Notion — nine months of meeting notes, her summaries, the interview synthesis documents she'd written in full and sent to the team on Monday mornings. [pause 1s] She did not email anything from her work account. She did not copy anything to personal cloud during office hours. [pause 0.5s] She used Veltara's approved offboarding export tool, which she had read about in the employee handbook fourteen months earlier and never forgotten.

[pause 2s]

[cold precision] On day twelve she submitted her resignation. Two weeks early. No explanation given. No mention of Marcus. No mention of the presentation. [pause 0.5s] Her resignation letter thanked Veltara for six years and wished the team well. [pause 1s] It was four sentences. All of them true.

[pause 2s]

[turning warm] She named the company Crestline UX LLC. [pause 0.5s] She registered it from the desk in her apartment — a standing desk she'd bought the week after resigning, positioned under the east window so she'd have light in the morning. [pause 1s] She built her portfolio in the first two weeks. Anonymized case studies. Process documentation. Interview synthesis samples. All of it hers. All of it dated.

[pause 2s]

[turning warm] The first call came six weeks after she launched her website. [pause 0.5s] Dana, from Meridian Freight. A logistics company running on a dashboard built in 2017 that nobody had loved since 2019. Dana had found Nadia through LinkedIn, through a comment Nadia had left on a U-X-P-A thread about user research methodology. [pause 0.5s] Dana's first question was: "Was the Orrin platform yours?" [pause 1s] Nadia said: "Most of it." [pause 1s] Dana said: "I thought so. The information architecture." [pause 0.5s] The engagement was seventy-four thousand dollars. Twelve-week scope.

[pause 2s]

[knowing] At Veltara, Priya in design operations had been doing a retrospective on the Orrin project — standard post-launch documentation required for the annual capability report. [pause 0.5s] She pulled the Figma files. She opened the version history. [pause 1s] She looked at it for a while. [pause 0.5s] Then she pulled the Notion meeting notes. Then she pulled the Slack thread archive. [pause 1s] Then she sent a message to her manager: "Did you know that ninety-four percent of the Orrin artboards are attributed to someone who no longer works here?"

[pause 2s]

[knowing] The U-X-P-A Regional Excellence Award nominations open in January. [pause 0.5s] Someone on the awards committee — the committee does not disclose who — had used the Orrin platform personally, in a client context, and had wondered about the design thinking behind it. [pause 1s] They searched the Figma Community. They found a process document Nadia had shared publicly six months before the project ended — an interview synthesis template, attributed to her name, timestamped, cross-referenced with the Orrin system's navigation logic. [pause 0.5s] The committee contacted Veltara requesting confirmation of authorship. [pause 1s] Veltara's response took eleven days.

[pause 2s]

[knowing] Meridian Freight's dashboard launched in Q2. Error rate dropped forty-one percent in the first ninety days. Dana sent Nadia a case study summary without being asked. [pause 0.5s] Nadia posted it to her portfolio. She added one line beneath the metrics: "Design is a record. Version history is forever."

[pause 2s]

[knowing] The Orrin Group's contract renewal was up in March. They had two vendors on their shortlist. One of them was not Veltara. [pause 1s] Orrin chose the other one. The reason given in the procurement summary: "Insufficient clarity on internal capability attribution." [pause 0.5s] Which is procurement language for: we looked at the files and we weren't sure who actually did the work.

[pause 2s]

[knowing] Marcus was moved lateral. Same title. Different team. No client-facing accounts. [pause 1s] The org chart updated on a Wednesday afternoon. By Thursday morning, his LinkedIn showed "Strategic Initiatives" where "Project Lead" had been.
```

## CHUNK 3 — Beat 8 + Epilogue + CTA (~420 words, ~2:55)

Paste NGUYÊN vào ElevenLabs:

```
[knowing] The thing about design work is that it leaves a record that has nothing to do with who presents it. [pause 1s] Every decision has a timestamp. Every revision has a name attached to it. Every interview framework, every artboard, every architecture choice — they don't disappear when someone else stands at the front of the room. [pause 0.5s] They sit in version history. Patient. Dated. Waiting for someone to open the panel. [pause 1s] Marcus had built a presentation. Nadia had built a platform. [pause 0.5s] Those are not the same thing, and eventually — not always, but eventually — the difference becomes visible to the people who need to see it.

[pause 2s]

[warm resolve] Crestline UX LLC closed its first year with seven clients and two employees — a junior researcher Nadia hired in September, and a part-time project coordinator she brought on in November. [pause 0.5s] Revenue for the quarter came in at three hundred and forty thousand dollars. [pause 1s] The U-X-P-A Regional Excellence Award was announced in April. Nadia attended the ceremony in a sage blazer. She sat in the third row. When they read her name, she stood up and walked to the front of the room and accepted the trophy — which is a small clear acrylic block, not dramatic, just solid — and she said three sentences into the microphone and sat back down. [pause 0.5s] Afterward, she put the trophy on the windowsill of the Crestline office. [pause 1s] East window. Morning light.

[pause 2s]

[warm resolve] She had stopped building for someone else. [pause 2s] That is, in the end, what the record showed. Not the presentation. Not the slide with her name in fourth position in a footnote. [pause 0.5s] The three hundred artboards. The fourteen interviews. The nine months of Mondays. [pause 1s] All of it timestamped. All of it hers. All of it sitting in version history whether anyone looked at it or not. [pause 1s] Someone always looks eventually.

[pause 2s]

[inviting] If you kept your files, if you kept your receipts, if you let the record speak when you couldn't — leave a butterfly in the comments. [pause 1s] Every butterfly is someone who understood that the most powerful thing you can do is document your work and let the version history be the witness. [pause 0.5s] Subscribe to Karma Caught Up — because the record has a longer memory than the presentation, and we will be here when it corrects itself. [pause 0.5s] We'll see you in the next one.
```
