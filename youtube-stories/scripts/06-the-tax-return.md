# Episode 06 — The Tax Return

> **Runtime**: 11:30 · **Protagonist**: Sarah (CPA)
> **Antagonist**: Daniel (restaurant owner) · **Third party**: Rachel (Daniel's bookkeeper)

---

## BEAT 1 — HOOK (0:00-0:15)

[knowing] Sarah filed her husband's taxes every April for nine years. [pause 1s] In the tenth year, she found a receipt for a hotel he'd never told her about. [pause 0.5s] Then she kept pulling the thread. And every knot she untied tightened around him.

[pause 2s]

## BEAT 2 — INTRO (0:15-1:00)

[warm] Sarah was thirty-seven. A Certified Public Accountant at a boutique firm in Chicago that handled high-net-worth families. Precise, patient, the kind of woman who enjoyed the music of reconciling ledgers on a Sunday afternoon. [pause 0.5s] Her husband Daniel owned a small restaurant. He hated paperwork. He hated numbers. He hated taxes. [pause 1s]

Which is why, every April, Sarah sat down at the kitchen table with Daniel's shoebox of receipts and a pot of coffee and did his business taxes for him. [pause 0.5s] She didn't mind. She was good at it. And she believed what the magazines said about marriage — that it was a partnership of complementary strengths. [pause 1s] That was her first mistake — assuming that when a partner hands you the receipts, they're handing you all the receipts.

[pause 2s]

## BEAT 3 — THE CRACK (1:00-2:30)

[foreboding] The crack appeared on a Saturday afternoon in April, exactly one week before the filing deadline. [pause 0.5s] Sarah was sorting Daniel's shoebox, entering expense categories into her spreadsheet. Office supplies. Gas. Lumber. Business meals. [pause 1s] She picked up a hotel receipt from the Marriott Magnificent Mile for a single night in August. Two hundred and forty-seven dollars. [pause 0.5s] The receipt had a handwritten note at the bottom: "client dinner." [pause 1s]

Sarah paused. Daniel owned a restaurant. A farm-to-table place in Wicker Park. He did not have clients who stayed at downtown hotels. He barely had suppliers who traveled at all. [pause 0.5s] She checked her spreadsheet from last year. [pause 1s] There were four Marriott receipts from the previous tax year she'd categorized as "business travel." [pause 0.5s] All for single nights. [pause 0.5s] All with the note "client dinner." [pause 1s]

Sarah opened Daniel's calendar app — he'd linked it to their family calendar years ago, so she always knew when he had a late job. [pause 0.5s] She cross-referenced the Marriott dates. [pause 1s] Every one of those nights, Daniel had told her he was "working late at the restaurant for a private event." [pause 2s]

She set down her coffee. [pause 0.5s] She opened the business bank account online. [pause 1s] She did what she did best. She ran a search by vendor. [pause 0.5s] And she pulled up every transaction to every hotel in Chicago for the past three years. [pause 1s] Twenty-three nights. Always Fridays. Always single rooms. Always marked "business meals" in his own handwriting on the receipts she filed every April. [pause 2s]

Sarah sat very still in her kitchen. [pause 0.5s] She was a CPA. She was used to finding errors in other people's ledgers. [pause 1s] She had just found twenty-three of them in her own.

[pause 2s]

## BEAT 4 — THE BETRAYAL (2:30-4:00) ⭐

[heavy] That night, Daniel was at a "job site walkthrough." [pause 0.5s] Sarah opened his business QuickBooks account — she had the login, because she'd set it up for him three years ago — and she began running reports. [pause 1s] She started with "payroll." [pause 2s]

Daniel had three employees on payroll: two sous chefs and a bookkeeper. [pause 0.5s] The bookkeeper's name was Rachel Mason. [pause 1s] Rachel had been on payroll for four years. Her salary was sixty thousand dollars a year — more than both sous chefs combined. [pause 0.5s] Which was strange, because Daniel had always told Sarah his business "didn't really need a bookkeeper," and because his business was small enough that Sarah had been doing the taxes herself. [pause 1s]

Sarah had never heard of Rachel Mason. [pause 2s]

She searched LinkedIn. Rachel Mason, thirty, based in Chicago. The profile listed her occupation as "freelance bookkeeper for small restaurants." It listed her current job as "Whitaker Kitchen" — Daniel's business. [pause 0.5s] Her profile photo was a confident blonde woman at a brunch. [pause 1s] Sarah scrolled down. [pause 0.5s] Rachel had a public Instagram tagged on the LinkedIn. [pause 1s]

Sarah opened the Instagram. [pause 0.5s] The most recent photo — posted four days ago — was a selfie in a hotel room. The Marriott Magnificent Mile. The same room Daniel had expensed in his latest receipt. [pause 1s] The caption said, "Business travel is looking up. 💼❤️" [pause 2s]

Sarah felt her stomach drop, but her hands were steady, because they always were when she was doing math. [pause 0.5s] She ran one more report. [pause 1s] Sarah pulled up Rachel's employment records in QuickBooks and checked when Rachel had first been added to payroll. [pause 0.5s] Four years ago. Three months after Sarah had filed Daniel's previous year's taxes. [pause 1s] The same month Sarah had finalized the mortgage on their house — the house that Daniel's "business income" had partially funded. [pause 2s]

Then Sarah ran the report she had been building toward. [pause 0.5s] Total dollars paid to Rachel Mason over four years. [pause 1s] Two hundred and forty thousand dollars. [pause 0.5s] Plus benefits. Plus twenty-three nights at the Marriott Magnificent Mile. [pause 1s]

Sarah's husband had been paying his girlfriend a full-time salary to be his mistress. [pause 0.5s] And Sarah had been filing the receipts for four tax years.

[pause 2s]

## BEAT 5 — THE FALL (4:00-5:30)

[heavy] Sarah closed the laptop. She made herself a sandwich. She ate it standing at the kitchen counter. [pause 1s] She thought about what she was going to do next. [pause 0.5s] Not emotionally. Procedurally. [pause 1s] Because Sarah was not the kind of woman who lost her head when she found an error. She was the kind of woman who wrote it down. [pause 2s]

Daniel came home at eleven p.m. smelling like cologne he had never worn when he'd met her. [pause 0.5s] He kissed the top of her head and said, "How are the taxes coming, babe? You're a saint for doing this." [pause 1s] Sarah smiled. "Almost done. Just a few categorizations left." [pause 0.5s] Daniel laughed. "You're too good for me." [pause 1s] Sarah said, very quietly, "Probably." [pause 0.5s] Daniel didn't catch the tone because he was already opening the refrigerator. [pause 2s]

For three days, Sarah continued to "work on the taxes." [pause 0.5s] She went to her office at work on her lunch breaks and made photocopies of everything. Every receipt. Every bank statement. Every payroll entry. Every email between Daniel and Rachel that she could find in his forgotten-open Gmail account. [pause 1s] She built a parallel ledger. Not for the IRS. For herself. [pause 0.5s] And for the lawyer she was about to call. [pause 2s]

On the fourth night, Sarah sat alone in her car in a grocery store parking lot after work. [pause 0.5s] Rain was falling on the windshield. She watched the drops collect and slide down the glass. [pause 1s] Then she picked up her phone, and she called a lawyer. [pause 2s]

## BEAT 6 — THE PIVOT (5:30-7:00) ⭐

[hopeful] The lawyer was a woman named Patricia. Patricia had worked for the IRS as an auditor for fifteen years before going into private practice in divorce law. [pause 0.5s] When Sarah explained that her husband had been paying his mistress as a fake employee — through a small restaurant where Sarah was the unpaid tax preparer — Patricia almost laughed. [pause 1s] "Sarah," she said, "this isn't a divorce case. This is a tax fraud case with a divorce attached. [pause 0.5s] Do you understand what this means?" [pause 1s]

Sarah said yes. [pause 0.5s] It meant that Daniel hadn't just cheated on her. He had committed a federal crime. [pause 1s] And because Sarah had signed every one of those joint returns, she was legally on the hook for half of that crime — unless she filed as an injured spouse first. [pause 0.5s] Patricia talked her through it. [pause 2s]

For one week, Sarah did the most meticulous work of her career. [pause 0.5s] She prepared a complete forensic accounting of Daniel's business for the past four years. Every expense. Every transfer. Every payroll entry. [pause 1s] She categorized each one honestly — not the way Daniel had categorized them, but the way a real auditor would. [pause 0.5s] She identified one hundred and eighty-three fraudulent entries. Total misreported income: about two hundred and eighty thousand dollars. [pause 1s]

Then she did two things on a Thursday morning. [pause 0.5s] First, she filed her own taxes separately as a married-filing-separately injured spouse, with a full disclosure of the discrepancies in her husband's business. This protected her legally. [pause 1s] Second, she printed a copy of her forensic report — all forty-seven pages — and put it in a manila envelope. [pause 0.5s] She addressed the envelope to the Internal Revenue Service, Chicago field office, Fraud Division. [pause 1s] She dropped it in the mail on her way to the airport. [pause 2s]

She had a flight to her sister's house in Portland booked for that afternoon. [pause 0.5s] Her suitcases were already in the car.

[pause 2s]

## BEAT 7 — KARMA STRIKES (7:00-9:30)

[satisfied] The IRS is a slow institution. [pause 0.5s] But it is also a thorough one. [pause 1s] Six weeks after Sarah's envelope arrived, Daniel received a letter from the IRS notifying him that his business was being audited. [pause 0.5s] Four days later, he received a second letter specifically regarding the employment of one Rachel Mason. [pause 1s] Daniel did what most people do when they receive a surprise audit letter. He panicked. [pause 2s]

He called Sarah. Her number was disconnected. [pause 0.5s] He called her sister in Portland. Her sister said Sarah was "unavailable." [pause 0.5s] He called Rachel Mason. Rachel, who had been tagged publicly in Sarah's reported Instagram post and had just that morning received a visit from a plainclothes IRS investigator at her apartment, was not taking his calls either. [pause 1s]

The IRS audit concluded eight months later. Daniel was ordered to repay two hundred and eighty thousand dollars in fraudulent deductions, plus penalties, plus interest. Total owed: about four hundred and ten thousand dollars. [pause 0.5s] He was also charged with willful tax fraud, which is a federal crime. [pause 1s] His attorney negotiated it down to a probation sentence instead of prison, but the felony stayed on his record. [pause 2s]

Meanwhile, Sarah's divorce — which Patricia had filed the same week as the IRS envelope — was rapid. Because Sarah had filed as an injured spouse and had whistle-blown the fraud herself, the court granted her protection from the debts. [pause 0.5s] Daniel got the tax bill. Sarah got the house, the legal retirement accounts, and a civil settlement for her unpaid labor as his tax preparer for nine years. [pause 1s]

Rachel Mason lost her bookkeeping license. Because she had knowingly accepted "salary" for work she had not performed, she was also investigated for tax fraud. She settled out of court and became, in her own words online, "unemployed and humbled." [pause 0.5s] Her LinkedIn no longer mentions Whitaker Kitchen. [pause 2s]

Daniel's business closed. The IRS placed liens on his kitchen equipment and his delivery van. [pause 0.5s] His two sous chefs — who had been legitimate employees, and who had watched Daniel hand checks to Rachel for four years without understanding what she was actually doing at the office — started their own small business together, taking Daniel's client list with them. [pause 1s] They sent Sarah a thank-you card when they made their first payroll. They had figured out who Sarah was. [pause 2s]

## BEAT 8 — THE DOWNFALL (9:30-10:30)

[final] Two years after the audit, Sarah received a certified letter from Daniel's attorney. [pause 0.5s] It was a formal request for a meeting regarding "a proposed payment plan for outstanding civil obligations." [pause 1s] Sarah forwarded the letter to Patricia. [pause 0.5s] Patricia wrote back with a single sentence: "Tell his attorney to contact the IRS directly. We are not involved in that negotiation." [pause 1s]

Daniel never sent another letter. [pause 0.5s] Last Sarah heard, he was working as a line cook at a diner in a different state. Not supervising. Just working. [pause 0.5s] He still owes the IRS about three hundred thousand dollars. [pause 1s] The IRS, unlike exes, does not forget.

## EPILOGUE (10:30-11:30)

[warm] Sarah is thirty-nine now. She made partner at her accounting firm last spring. Her office has a window that looks out at Lake Michigan. On her desk she keeps a small framed card that says "Thank you for showing us what a real ledger looks like." It was from Daniel's two sous chefs. She kept it. [pause 1s]

Last autumn, at a charity gala for financial literacy, Sarah met a man named James. He was a tax attorney for nonprofits. [pause 0.5s] They bonded immediately over a shared, deeply unfashionable love of filing systems.
