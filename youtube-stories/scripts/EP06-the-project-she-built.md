### BEAT 2 — PROTAGONIST INTRO (0:15–1:00) — 147 words

**[warm]**

This is Nadia. She was 32 at the time. A UX designer at a mid-sized software company called Veltara — the kind of firm that builds enterprise dashboards nobody loves but everybody uses. [pause 0.5s] She'd been there six years. Hired fresh out of graduate school, promoted twice, the person the junior designers went to when they were stuck. [pause 1s]

She was good. Not in a loud way. In a way that showed up in the work. [pause 0.5s] Her previous project — a complete redesign of Veltara's client onboarding portal — had reduced user drop-off by 34% in the first quarter after launch. That number lived in a slide deck the CEO used at conferences. [pause 0.5s] Nadia's name was not in that slide deck. [pause 1s] She'd noticed. But she'd let it go. [pause 0.5s] That was not her first mistake. It was just the first one she'd remember later.

### BEAT 3 — THE CRACK (1:00–2:30) — 258 words

**[foreboding]**

In January, Veltara landed its largest client to date — a logistics company called Orrin Group, with nine hundred employees across four countries. [pause 0.5s] The project: build a custom internal analytics platform from scratch. [pause 0.5s] Something that would talk to Orrin's existing systems, surface data their operations team could actually read, and do it in eight months. [pause 1s]

Nadia was assigned to the project. [pause 0.5s] Her manager was a man named Marcus. Thirty-nine. Senior Director of Product. The kind of man who was very good in rooms — confident voice, easy handshake, the ability to make whatever he said sound like it had already been agreed upon. [pause 1s]

Marcus ran the meetings. Nadia ran the project. [pause 1s]

She spent February doing user interviews with fourteen Orrin operations managers. She built the information architecture in Figma. She ran three rounds of usability testing, recruited the participants herself, wrote every test script. [pause 0.5s] She worked until 11 PM on four consecutive Thursdays to hit the milestone Marcus had committed to in a meeting she hadn't been invited to. [pause 1s]

She noticed small things. [pause 0.5s] Marcus would sit in her design reviews, take notes, and then send recap emails to the director team — using her exact words — without mentioning her name. [pause 0.5s] She noticed when a slide deck she'd built appeared in a leadership presentation, her name replaced with his in the footer. [pause 1s] She mentioned it once, lightly. He said he'd "fix it." [pause 0.5s] He didn't. [pause 0.5s] She told herself it was an oversight.

### BEAT 4 — THE BETRAYAL (2:30–4:00) — 278 words

**[whispers]**

The Orrin platform launched in September. [pause 0.5s] On time. Under budget. [pause 1s] Orrin's operations director sent a company-wide email calling it the most intuitive internal tool his team had ever used. [pause 1s]

Marcus gave a forty-minute presentation to the executive team. [pause 0.5s] Nadia was in the room. She sat at the end of the table and watched slide after slide — slides she had built, frameworks she had designed, user insights she had synthesized — presented in his voice. [pause 1s] Her name appeared once, in a slide titled "Project Contributors," in a list of eleven people. [pause 0.5s] She was fourth. [pause 2s]

Marcus received a Director-level bonus that quarter. [pause 0.5s] Nadia received a card signed by her teammates and a thirty-dollar gift certificate to a restaurant near the office. [pause 1s]

But that was not the moment. [pause 1s] The moment came two weeks later. [pause 0.5s] Marcus went to Veltara's VP of People — a woman named Gretchen — and raised "concerns" about Nadia. [pause 0.5s] Her communication style, he said. Her tendency to go around him on decisions. Her "resistance to feedback." [pause 1s] None of it documented. All of it delivered in the language of concern. [pause 0.5s] He said he was "trying to help her grow." [pause 2s]

Nadia found out from a peer review form that came back with language she'd never heard used about her before. [pause 0.5s] She requested a meeting with Gretchen. [pause 0.5s] Gretchen said the feedback was anonymous. [pause 0.5s] Then Gretchen told her that her role was being "restructured." [pause 1s] Her position eliminated. [pause 0.5s] Effective in thirty days.

### BEAT 5 — THE FALL (4:00–5:30) — 228 words

**[heavy]**

Nadia drove home that night on autopilot. [pause 0.5s] Sat in her car in the parking garage for twenty minutes before she could make herself go inside. [pause 1s]

She thought about the fourteen user interviews. [pause 0.5s] The test sessions she'd run at 8 AM before Marcus arrived. The Figma file that held three hundred artboards, every screen she'd designed, every annotation she'd written. [pause 1s] She thought about the version history — Figma logged every single change, every author, every timestamp. [pause 0.5s] She thought about the Slack threads, the Notion docs, the weekly standups she'd led, the emails she'd sent at 11:47 PM with subject lines like "Orrin — flow update for review." [pause 1s]

She still had all of it. [pause 1s] She always kept meticulous records — not because she'd ever imagined needing them, but because that was how she worked. Clean folders. Organized archives. [pause 0.5s] Version history saved automatically, timestamped, attributed. [pause 1s] She exported everything. Not as a threat. [pause 0.5s] Not as a weapon. [pause 0.5s] Just because it was hers, and she had built it, and she would not leave it behind. [pause 2s]

She submitted her resignation on day twelve of the thirty-day notice. [pause 0.5s] Wrote a professional email. Thanked the team. [pause 0.5s] Didn't say a single word about Marcus. [pause 1s] She closed the laptop and made herself dinner. [pause 0.5s] She had no idea what she was going to do next. [pause 1s] But she had the files.

### BEAT 6 — THE PIVOT (5:30–7:00) — 318 words

**[calm finality]**

The first month was quiet. She freelanced — small UX audits, a contract here, a Figma cleanup project there. [pause 0.5s] Enough to pay rent. Not enough to feel like anything. [pause 1s]

But somewhere around month two, something shifted. [pause 0.5s] She started looking at the Orrin files she'd saved — not out of bitterness, but because she'd noticed a problem in the platform she'd never had time to fix. [pause 0.5s] A gap in how the data was being surfaced for the logistics managers on the night shift. A workflow they were still doing manually that the platform should have automated. [pause 1s]

She built the fix. On her own. In a week. [pause 0.5s] Just to see if she could. [pause 1s]

Then she kept going. [pause 0.5s] She registered an LLC — Crestline UX, a small consultancy. One person. Her apartment office, a standing desk she'd bought secondhand, and a Notion board with seventeen potential clients she'd met over six years at Veltara. [pause 1s]

She did not badmouth Marcus. Did not post about what had happened. Did not go on LinkedIn with a vague "sometimes doors close for a reason" caption. [pause 0.5s] She just reached out to people she'd actually worked with. Quietly. One at a time. [pause 1s]

Three weeks into this, her phone rang. [pause 0.5s] It was a woman named Dana. Operations director at a supply chain company called Meridian Freight. They'd met two years earlier at a UX conference — a quick hallway conversation, a card exchanged. [pause 0.5s] Dana had remembered her specifically. Not Veltara. Not Marcus. [pause 0.5s] Her. [pause 1s]

"We've been trying to fix our driver dispatch interface for eight months," Dana said. "Nobody's gotten it right. I remembered that platform you described at that conference — the Orrin one. Was that yours?" [pause 2s]

Nadia paused. [pause 1s] "Yes," she said. "That was mine."

**[knowing]**

[pause 1s] The dispatch interface she built reduced driver check-in errors by 41% in the first sixty days. Dana's team sent her a bottle of champagne and a handwritten note that said: "Where have you been all our lives?" [pause 1s]

Dana referred her to two other clients. [pause 0.5s] Both in logistics. Both with the same problem — enterprise data that nobody could read without a spreadsheet degree. [pause 0.5s] Nadia could read it. More than that, she could make it legible to people who just needed to do their jobs. [pause 1s]

By month six, Crestline UX had four active clients. By month nine, she brought on a junior designer. [pause 1s]

Meanwhile, she started hearing things. [pause 0.5s] Not from anyone she'd told what happened — from people in the industry who'd figured it out on their own. [pause 1s]

A new hire at Veltara — a designer named Priya, fresh from a top program in Boston — had been assigned to work with Marcus on a new platform project. [pause 0.5s] Priya, being the kind of meticulous person Nadia recognized immediately, had pulled up the Orrin project files for reference. [pause 0.5s] She'd found the Figma version history. [pause 0.5s] She'd seen who had authored every single screen. [pause 1s] Every flow. Every component. [pause 0.5s] Nadia's name on 94% of the artboards. [pause 1s]

Priya mentioned it to a colleague. The colleague mentioned it to a friend. [pause 0.5s] The friend was connected to three people who knew Nadia. [pause 1s]

The industry is small. It always is. [pause 1s]

The moment that closed the ledger came in the spring. [pause 0.5s] Nadia was shortlisted for the UXPA Regional Excellence Award — specifically for the Meridian Freight dispatch system. [pause 0.5s] The nomination cited her "demonstrated ability to translate complex operational data into human-centered interfaces." [pause 1s] The awards committee contacted Veltara to verify her prior work history. [pause 0.5s] The Figma file came up. [pause 0.5s] Version history doesn't lie. [pause 2s]

Marcus was not nominated for anything that year. Or the year after. [pause 1s] Orrin Group quietly declined to renew their contract with Veltara when it came up for renewal. [pause 0.5s] No explanation given. [pause 0.5s] But Dana had made a call. And Dana knew people. [pause 1s] Nadia had not asked her to. She hadn't asked anyone to do anything. [pause 0.5s] That was never the point.

### BEAT 8 — THE DOWNFALL (9:30–10:30) — 187 words

**[calm finality]**

About a year after Nadia left Veltara, she heard that Marcus had been moved laterally — same title, smaller team, no client-facing work. [pause 0.5s] The kind of quiet repositioning companies do when they can't fire someone without paperwork but don't want to promote them either. [pause 1s]

She heard it from someone who still worked there. [pause 0.5s] Not gossip — just news. The way you hear that a road you used to take every day has been rerouted. [pause 1s]

She didn't feel the rush of satisfaction she might have expected. [pause 0.5s] What she felt instead was something quieter. [pause 0.5s] Something closer to relief. [pause 1s] Not because he'd suffered — she didn't know if he had. [pause 0.5s] But because the work had spoken. [pause 1s] Figma's version history had spoken. Dana's call had spoken. The UXPA nomination had spoken. [pause 1s]

None of it required her to say a single word. [pause 0.5s] She'd built the thing. [pause 0.5s] The record showed who'd built it. [pause 1s] That was always going to be true. She just had to wait.

**[warm]**

She won the UXPA Regional Excellence Award. [pause 0.5s] The trophy sits on the windowsill of her home office, next to her standing desk and a very good plant she has managed not to kill for two years. [pause 1s]

The betrayal didn't break her. [pause 0.5s] It just made her stop building for someone else. [pause 1s] Everything she has built since then — she put her own name on. [pause 0.5s] And it turned out she was very, very good at building things. [pause 1s] She always had been.
